# Pitch Storytelling Guide

Reference for crafting investor pitch narratives. Every principle here should directly influence slide content.
When generating or reviewing slides, check each point against this guide.

---

## 1. The Narrative Arc

Every strong pitch follows a seven-beat structure. Each beat maps to one or more slides:

1. **Shift** — "The world is changing because..."
   Open with the macro trend or technological shift that creates the opportunity. This is not your company story; it is the context that makes your company inevitable. Use concrete data: a market inflection point, a regulatory change, a technology breakthrough with a specific date.

2. **Problem** — "This creates pain for..."
   Name the specific people who suffer and how. Quantify the cost of the status quo. The audience must feel the problem before hearing any solution. Use a real user quote if available. The best problem slides make the investor nod in recognition.

3. **Insight** — "We discovered that..."
   The non-obvious truth your team uniquely understands. This is the intellectual foundation of the company. If anyone could have seen it, explain why no one acted. This slide separates fundable companies from feature requests.

4. **Solution** — "So we built..."
   Show the product. One slide, one demo screenshot, one clear value proposition. Connect it directly back to the pain described earlier. The solution should feel like the inevitable response to the problem.

5. **Proof** — "It's working because..."
   Traction, revenue, user love, retention curves. Real data that demonstrates product-market fit or early signal of it. Even pre-revenue companies can show proof: waitlist size, pilot results, LOIs, user engagement depth.

6. **Vision** — "This leads to..."
   The larger market you expand into once the wedge is established. Paint the picture of the company at scale. This is where you earn the right to show a large TAM — after proving you can capture the small one.

7. **Ask** — "To get there, we need..."
   Funding amount, use of proceeds, key milestones the capital unlocks. Be specific: "We are raising $3M to hire 5 engineers and reach $1M ARR in 18 months."

The arc is sequential. Do not skip steps or reorder. Problem before solution. Proof before vision. Always.

## 2. YC Principles

Derived from YC office hours methodology and partner feedback patterns:

- **Desperate specificity beats vague claims.** "Teachers with 3+ years who run after-school tutoring centers in Tier-2 cities" is stronger than "educators." Narrow the audience until it feels almost too small — that is the right size for a seed pitch.

- **Show, don't tell.** Revenue beats "we plan to monetize." A retention chart beats "users love us." A customer quote beats "strong product-market fit." Replace every adjective with a data point.

- **Narrowest wedge first.** Who are the first 100 users? Not the first 100,000 — the first 100. Be painfully specific about the entry point, the use case, and why those users cannot live without you. The wedge strategy also reveals the go-to-market plan.

- **"What do you know that others don't?"** This is the single most important question. The unique insight — from domain expertise, user research, or technical capability — that makes this team the right one to build this company. If the insight is obvious, the pitch is weak.

- **Talk to users, not investors.** The best decks reflect real conversations with real customers. Use their language, their pain descriptions, their metrics. A deck built from user interviews is always stronger than one built from market reports.

- **Legibility over cleverness.** If a slide needs explanation, rewrite it. Every slide should communicate its point in under 5 seconds of reading. Clever metaphors and complex diagrams fail in a 3-minute pitch.

- **Founder-market fit is implicit.** Do not state "we are uniquely qualified." Demonstrate it through the insight, the product decisions, and the traction. Let the investor conclude it themselves.

- **One idea per slide.** If you find yourself saying "and also" on a slide, split it. Density kills comprehension in a pitch setting.

## 3. a16z Framework

Key principles from a16z's investment evaluation lens:

- **Start with Why Now.** What changed in the last 12-24 months? A new technology (LLMs reaching production quality), a regulation shift (data privacy laws), a behavior change (remote work normalization). If the answer is "nothing changed," the timing argument is weak. The "why now" must be external to the company.

- **Problem must be visceral.** Quote a real user. Show the pain with a specific anecdote, not an abstract description. "I spend 6 hours every week copying data between three spreadsheets" beats "workflow inefficiency is a major challenge." If the founder has not talked to users, this slide will feel hollow.

- **Solution equals demo.** If you cannot convey the product on one slide with one screenshot or diagram, the product story is too complicated. Simplify until a single image tells the story. Annotate the screenshot — do not assume the investor sees what you see.

- **Traction is the strongest signal.** Any metric that trends up and to the right matters more than any promise. Revenue, active users, retention rate, NPS — choose the metric that best reflects real value creation and feature it prominently. A single strong chart can carry an entire pitch.

- **Market means pull, not TAM.** Do not lead with "the market is $50B." Instead show demand: waitlists, inbound interest, organic growth, channel partner requests. Pull from the market is more convincing than a top-down TAM calculation. Save the big number for after you have demonstrated pull.

- **Team equals unfair advantage.** Why THIS team, specifically? Previous exits, domain expertise measured in years, unique access to distribution, technical breakthroughs the team authored. Generic bios do not persuade. Highlight what each person brings that is hard to replicate.

- **Competitive positioning is about insight, not features.** A feature comparison matrix where you win every column is not credible. Instead, explain the strategic difference: what you believe about the market that competitors do not. Frame competition as validation of the market, then explain your differentiated bet.

## 4. Data Presentation Rules

How to present numbers, charts, and evidence on slides:

- **One number per stat card.** Do not cram multiple figures into one visual element. "4.35亿" is one card. "3x YoY growth" is a separate card. White space around numbers makes them land harder.

- **Always cite sources.** Bottom of the slide, small text (use the `.source` class), format: "Source: iResearch, 2025" or "Source: Company internal data, March 2026." Uncited numbers lose credibility instantly.

- **Growth rates beat absolute numbers at early stage.** "3x YoY" is more impressive than "$500K ARR" for a seed-stage company. Absolute numbers matter at Series B and beyond. Match the metric type to the stage.

- **Comparisons create context.** "Harvey raised at $11B to serve lawyers alone — we serve all knowledge workers" is more powerful than stating your TAM. Anchor to something the investor already values. Good comparisons: competitor valuations, incumbent spend, user time saved vs. current workflow.

- **Round aggressively.** Investors do not care about $4,347,291. Say "$4.3M." Do not care about 43,891 users. Say "44K users." Precision signals spreadsheet-thinking, not storytelling. Save exact figures for the data room.

- **Use `.beat-num` class** for key metrics that should animate with a count-up effect on the slide. Reserve this for the 2-3 most important numbers in the deck — overuse dilutes impact.

- **Time horizon creates credibility.** "Revenue doubled in 4 years (2022-2026)" with a visible timeline is stronger than "revenue doubled." Show the trajectory, not just the endpoint. Include axis labels and year markers.

- **Charts need a punchline.** Every chart should have a one-line annotation that tells the investor what to conclude. Do not rely on them to interpret the data correctly. Place the annotation above or below the chart, not buried in a legend.

- **Avoid pie charts.** Bar charts and line charts communicate magnitude and trend. Pie charts communicate nothing quickly. Use horizontal bars for comparisons, line charts for growth over time.

- **Three stats per proof slide, maximum.** A wall of numbers is noise. Select the three strongest data points and let them breathe. If you have more strong metrics, use a second slide.

## 5. Common Mistakes

Patterns that weaken a pitch — flag these when reviewing or generating deck content:

- **Too many slides.** Seed round: 12-16 slides. Series A: 16-20. Beyond that, trim ruthlessly. Every slide must earn its place. If removing a slide does not hurt the story, it should not exist.

- **No "why now?" explanation.** If the company could have been started five years ago, the investor wonders why it was not. Address timing explicitly. "Why now" is one of the top three questions investors ask.

- **Team slide as afterthought.** At seed stage, the team slide is often the most important. Place it where it supports the narrative — usually after the insight or after the proof section, not buried at the end.

- **Unbelievable competitive matrix.** A 2x2 or feature grid where you win every single category signals either dishonesty or lack of self-awareness. Acknowledge where competitors are strong and explain your differentiated bet.

- **Financial projections without unit economics.** A hockey-stick revenue chart without CAC, LTV, or gross margin is fantasy. Show the engine, not just the output. Investors want to see that you understand the economics of each customer.

- **Solution before problem.** The investor must feel the pain before hearing the cure. If the first substantive slide is your product, restructure. Pain creates the context that makes the solution compelling.

- **Jargon and buzzwords.** "Leveraging AI-powered synergies to optimize cross-functional workflows" communicates nothing. "AI helps tutors serve 10x more students at the same cost" communicates everything. Use plain language. Test: would a smart friend outside your industry understand this slide?

- **No clear ask.** State the raise amount, intended use of funds, and what milestones the capital will unlock. "We're raising" without specifics suggests the founder has not thought it through.

- **Vanity metrics over value metrics.** Page views, downloads, registered users — these are vanity. Revenue, monthly active usage, retention at day-30, NPS — these are value. Always choose value metrics.

- **Logo soup without context.** A slide full of customer logos means nothing without annotation. "12 enterprise customers, $45K average contract" is the context that makes logos meaningful.

## 6. Bilingual Tips

For decks that present in both Chinese and English:

- **Rewrite, do not translate.** English content should read as natural English, not as translated Chinese. Restructure sentences entirely. A Chinese phrase like "赋能千行百业" should become "serving enterprises across industries," not "empowering thousands of trades and hundreds of industries."

- **Number format conversion.** Chinese large-number units do not map to English ones. Convert explicitly:
  - 4.35亿 = 435M
  - 3100万 = 31M
  - 1.2万亿 = 1.2T
  - Do not leave 亿/万 in English slides. Do not leave M/B in Chinese slides.

- **Visual weight balance.** Both languages should occupy roughly equal space on a bilingual slide. If the Chinese text is 15 characters and the English is 40 words, the English is too long — tighten it. Aim for the English to be concise enough that neither language dominates visually.

- **Proper nouns stay in original language.** Brand names (McKinsey, 华为), product names, and person names remain as-is. Do not translate "OpenAI" to Chinese or "字节跳动" to English unless adding a parenthetical for clarity.

- **Technical terms commonly used in English stay in English** even on Chinese slides: AI, SaaS, ARR, CAC, LTV, API, NPS, ROI. Translating these adds confusion, not clarity. The target audience for a pitch deck knows these terms.

- **Date formats.** Chinese: "2026年3月." English: "March 2026" or "Mar 2026." Never "2026/03" in a pitch deck — too clinical. Consistency matters: pick one format per language and use it throughout.

- **Currency conventions.** $1M is universally understood. RMB amounts should use the ¥ symbol. On bilingual slides, maintain the currency relevant to the audience context. For Chinese investors seeing USD amounts, parenthetical RMB equivalents can help: "$10M (约7000万元)."

- **Sentence structure differs.** Chinese often front-loads context ("在AI快速发展的背景下..."), while English prefers leading with the point ("AI adoption is accelerating, creating..."). Respect each language's natural flow rather than mirroring structure.
